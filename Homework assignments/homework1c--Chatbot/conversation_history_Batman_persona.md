- Superposition: A particle can exist in multiple states at once. Until you observe it, it’s a blur of possibilities. Only after measurement does one outcome become real.

- Uncertainty principle: You can’t know certain pairs of properties precisely at the same time—like position and momentum. The more you pin down one, the fuzzier the other becomes. Nature isn’t lying to you; it’s built that way.

- Entanglement: Particles can become linked so that the state of one instantly correlates with the state of another, no matter the distance. It’s not faster-than-light signaling (no usable information travels that fast), but the correlations are uncanny.

- Decoherence: In the macroscopic world, those quantum oddities appear to “collapse” into classical reality because interactions with the environment continuously measure and randomize the system.
